# Thesis Proposal Report
## Formalization and Its Applications Based on AI and Lean

### 1. Research Objectives and Significance
#### 1.1 Research Background
Modern mathematics is becoming more complex. As the field expands and abstract theories grow, the length and logical complexity of proofs are increasing rapidly. Traditionally, the mathematical community has relied on peer review to verify the correctness of academic results. However, modern mathematics has reached a point where reviewers often cannot check every logical step within a limited time. Consequently, the community often relies on "collective trust" to maintain the academic system. To solve this problem, mathematics is shifting from traditional *Informal Mathematics* to *Formal Mathematics*, which is verified by a computer's logical kernel.

Formal verification removes the ambiguity of natural language by using mathematical logic with strict definitions. Computer algorithms then ensure that every step of a proof follows specific rules. Among various Interactive Theorem Provers (ITPs), Lean has become a central focus. Developed in 2013 by Leonardo de Moura, Lean is based on Dependent Type Theory and the Curry-Howard Isomorphism. This theory states that "propositions are types, and proofs are programs." Therefore, Lean is both a proof assistant and a functional programming language that supports metaprogramming.

Although formal tools are reliable, manual formalization is slow and difficult. Even experts spend much time handling technical details like type mismatches and implicit arguments when translating textbook proofs into Lean code. For example, mathematicians often assume the Cartesian product is associative and switch between different dimensions without explanation. However, in a formal system, the computer lacks this intuition. Researchers must explicitly define these relationships and prove their equivalence before moving to the main theorem. This "semantic gap" between natural language and machine code slows down the development of formal libraries and limits its wider use [1][3].

As a result, more researchers are exploring the use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in mathematics. Large Language Models (LLMs) show great potential in mathematical reasoning and code generation. Tools like automated provers and Copilot are becoming more effective at suggesting proof steps and verifying logical chains. Fields Medalist Terence Tao has predicted that humans will soon interact directly with AI to formalize proofs into Lean code [4].

#### 1.2 Current Research Status
**1. Foundations: Lean and Mathlib**
Mathlib is the main mathematical library for Lean. By mid-2025, it contained over 210,000 machine-verified theorems and 100,000 definitions. It covers major areas of pure mathematics at the undergraduate and graduate levels, including algebra, topology, analysis, and probability [5]. Its integrated design ensures logical consistency and provides a solid foundation for formalizing complex theorems.

**2. Progress in Frontier Mathematics**
Top mathematicians have recently led several major projects in Lean. For example, Peter Scholze’s "Liquid Tensor Experiment" (2019) verified a complex theorem in condensed mathematics in just 18 months. This proved Lean's ability to handle advanced abstract theories. Later, Terence Tao and his team formalized the Polynomial Freiman-Ruzsa (PFR) conjecture using Lean 4. This project also expanded Mathlib's capabilities in areas like information theory [9]. Kevin Buzzard is also leading a project to fully formalize the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem [7]. These achievements show that Lean is mature enough for large-scale mathematical research.

**3. AI and Formal Mathematics**
Many studies are integrating AI with Lean to speed up formalization. Tools like LeanCopilot (Caltech) have embedded LLMs into Lean, automating about 74% of proof steps [10]. Morph Prover models have also improved the accuracy of proof generation [11]. In reasoning, DeepMind's AlphaProof reached a silver-medal level at the 2024 International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO). This logic is now part of general models like Gemini [12]. Additionally, projects like Formal Abstracts are creating machine-readable indices of papers to support mathematical machine learning [13].

**4. Domestic Progress**
Chinese researchers have also made significant progress. The Numina-Lean-Agent system, developed by the Chinese Academy of Sciences and Numina, successfully formalized all 12 problems of the 2025 Putnam Competition. Its performance matches top international systems [14].

ByteDance’s Seed team developed the Seed Prover series for difficult math competitions. Its latest version, Seed Prover 1.5, uses an agent-based architecture to break complex problems into smaller, reusable parts. It can search Mathlib and run Python scripts for verification. In the 2025 IMO, it generated verifiable Lean code for the first five problems in 16.5 hours, reaching a gold-medal score. It also solved 88% of problems in a Putnam test set within 9 hours [15].

#### 1.3 Research Objectives

1. Master the syntax, proof strategies, and design of Mathlib in Lean 4.

4. Create a Lean 4 teaching plan for undergraduate math courses and explore its use in education.
